## 1. The Lamp Room

Mara climbed the ninety-seven iron steps the way she had every evening since she was twelve, one hand
trailing the cold rail, the other cupped around a small brass lantern that had belonged to her
grandmother. At the top of the lighthouse the wind always found her first, tugging at her dark braid and
the worn green coat she never bothered to button.

The lamp room smelled of oil and salt. Mara set her lantern on the little table beside the great lens
and began the work she could do with her eyes closed: trimming the wick, checking the clockwork, winding
the weight that turned the light through the night. Below her, the village of Saltcombe glimmered, a
handful of yellow windows against the dark water.

Her grandmother used to say the sea kept its promises in tides, not in words. Mara was beginning to
understand what she had meant. Tonight the water was unusually still, the kind of stillness that, in her
experience, came just before the sea asked for something back.

## 2. The Stranger on the Quay

In the morning a man was waiting on the quay, soaked to the knees, holding a battered leather satchel
against his chest as though it might float away. He introduced himself as Edrin, a cartographer, and
said he had come to map the coast before the autumn storms erased the old paths for good.

Mara did not trust mapmakers. Maps made the sea look solved, and the sea was never solved. But Edrin had
kind, tired eyes, and he asked her questions no visitor had asked before — not about the light, but about
the people the light had failed to bring home. So she let him sit in her grandmother's chair, and she
poured him tea from the dented copper pot, and she listened.

He unrolled a chart on the table, weighting its corners with stones, and traced a line with his finger
to a cove she knew well: Hollow Reach, where the rocks showed their teeth at low water. "There's
something out there," he said quietly. "Something the old charts agree to forget."

## 3. Hollow Reach

They went at the turning of the tide, when the sea draws its breath and holds it. Mara rowed; Edrin
read the water. The brass lantern sat between them, unlit, a small bright weight.

At Hollow Reach the rocks rose like the ribs of a sunken thing, and between them, where no map had ever
admitted, stood the stub of an older lighthouse — black, salt-eaten, far more ancient than her own.
Mara's grandmother had never spoken of it. And yet, carved above its ruined door, worn almost smooth,
was the same spiral mark that decorated the base of Mara's lantern.

"You knew," Edrin said. It was not quite a question.

Mara looked from the carving to the lantern and felt the years rearrange themselves quietly behind her.
The sea had kept its promise after all. It had simply taken its time.

She stepped onto the black rock, lantern in hand, and for the first time in her life she struck the
match before the dark asked her to.
